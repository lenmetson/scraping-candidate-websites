Aberdeen North - boundary changes

The Westminster constituency of Aberdeen North will be different for this General Election. Dyce, Danestone and parts of the Bridge of Don and Bucksburn have moved from Gordon to Aberdeen North.

There are also some smaller boundary changes in the city centre with several areas moving from Aberdeen North to South.

It's neck and neck in our constituency

The SNP won Aberdeen North for the first time in 2015. Prior to then, the constituency had been solid Labour from 1935, represented by respected politicians such as Bob Hughes and Frank Doran.

According to recent Scotland-wide polls (see poll from this May on the left), and from the thousands of voters we've spoken to in the area, the SNP vote is going down and the Conservatives and Liberal Democrats are too far behind to win here.

Aberdeen North will be a two horse race between Labour and the SNP. 

Every vote counts - YOUR vote counts

Very recent polls shows that Scots are hungry for change and are turning to the Labour Party to deliver that change. The road to a Westminster Labour government starts right here in Scotland. Vote Labour and don't just "send a message", send a Government!

Across Scotland, Labour leads the SNP, but in Aberdeen North, we have a large SNP majority to overturn.

The only party that can beat the SNP here is Labour, so if you want change, you need to vote Labour.

We're taking nothing for granted

My team has been out campaigning for months now. We've delivered tens of thousands of leaflets and knocked on thousands of doors. We've had lots of conversations with voters and shown them how the Labour party has listened, reflected and changed. 

We have a once in a lifetime opportunity to have a Labour government, and it would be an honour if you were to lend me your vote.

    Council meeting 11 October 2023: Lynn speaking in debate about poverty

     At Aberdeen City Council today, Lynn spoke in debate about the impact of poverty and asked the SNP/LibDem administration to fund warm winter clothing for children instead of paying £50,000 to set up a Citizens Assembly to discuss poverty.  Citizens Assemblies do have their place, but we already have a lot of information at our fingertips and the time to act is now.
